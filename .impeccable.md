## Design Context

### Users
Small business owners, startup founders, and HR leads at early-stage companies (1–100 employees). They're wearing multiple hats — hiring is one of their most important jobs but not their only one. They use the dashboard daily or weekly during active hiring cycles. On the public site, they're evaluating KiteHR against paid alternatives and need to feel confident this free tool is worth their trust.

### Brand Personality
**Bold, ambitious, startup-native.** KiteHR should feel like the tool a fast-moving founder would build for themselves — sharp, direct, no bloat. Not a corporate HR system. Not a generic SaaS. Something with a point of view.

### Emotional Goal
**Energized and motivated.** Opening the dashboard should feel like sitting down at a tool that respects your time and makes you feel capable. Not clinical, not overwhelming — propulsive.

### Reference / Anti-reference
- **Reference**: Stripe — sophisticated, precise, premium but accessible. Typographically confident. Uses white space and restraint to feel authoritative, not flashy.
- **Anti-reference**: Generic AI-built SaaS landing pages — dark backgrounds, cyan/neon accents, glowing blurs, gradient text on headlines, glassmorphism cards. This is the exact aesthetic KiteHR currently has and should move away from.

### Aesthetic Direction
Move toward **refined boldness**: strong typographic hierarchy, intentional color, minimal decorative noise. The homepage should feel like Stripe meets a modern hiring tool — confident without relying on glows or gradients. The dashboard should carry the same DNA: a bit of personality and warmth, not a vanilla gray template.

### Design Principles
1. **Typographic confidence over decorative effects** — let the words carry weight; don't lean on gradient text or glow to manufacture impact.
2. **Color with meaning** — every color choice should communicate something (status, priority, action), not just look energetic.
3. **Continuity between homepage and dashboard** — the brand personality shouldn't reset when the user logs in.
4. **Restraint as a differentiator** — at a time when every AI tool looks the same, doing less more precisely stands out.
5. **Prove the product, don't describe it** — show real pipelines, real metrics, real outcomes rather than generic feature lists.
